My view on the Crusades:

It started as a defensive response. It did spiral into something horrific with massacres and land grabs and power plays. The Crusades were brutal, I'm not gonna argue that, like no way am I defending the Catholic Church's history, I wish things could have gone down a lot better.

My actual point is more specific than that I suppose: Today looks a lot different than back then, the more war prone verses of the bible are often ignored, or serve little relevancy today. Modern Christianity, for most people, is more of a moral compass, like... People quote the better parts of the bible, like love thy neighbor, show compassion like Jesus did, forgive those who wrong you, help the poor, don't judge others lest you be judged yourself..  They use religion to be better people and to better our culture.

But I must think about what life was like before, during, or shortly after the crusades, like imagine someone sitting in a cottage house without internet or youtube or any friends across the world to bounce ideas off of. Most people couldn't even read. The Church was literally the only institution that taught literacy, monasteries and cathedral schools were the only schools that existed, and the Bible was the primary text they used to teach reading and writing. For people who couldn't read, which was most people, they learned the Bible's stories through sermons, or through the stained glass windows and paintings in churches, which were literally called "scripture for the illiterate." The Church wasn't just religion, it was the entire education system and the entire media rolled into one. This giant tome full of mythology and moral guiding stories was the center of everything. It was their entertainment, their education, their culture's entire social framework. They had nothing else to cross reference it against, not like we have today, there was no real way to fact check the pope.

And even back then, the Bible still gave people a moral baseline that they wouldn't have had otherwise. Before this book that held cultural myths everyone agreed to learn from, people were in raw survival mode. Murder, theft, tribal warfare over resources, no shared ethical framework beyond their small tribal thinking, we weren't forming proper societies or any proper countries without it. The Bible and the literacy it inspired gave entire populations a common set of rules. Don't kill, don't steal, treat others how you want to be treated. For all its flaws, it was the first moral operating system that scaled across cultures and held people accountable to something, this father figure in the sky watching over you.

And that mattered because before that, the only law was whoever had the biggest army or the sharpest sword. Might is right, that's it. This idea of a father figure in the heavens had us believing there is something above raw power, that even the king of our country answers to a higher moral authority. It's a massive shift in our history. Suddenly there were principles that transcended raw force, and people actually internalized them.

That sense that there's something greater out there is still alive today, even in most non religious people, but it's an energy that religion harnessed and scaled across civilizations. Christianity took that and spread it to entire continents worth of people who otherwise had nothing holding them together.

To me the Bible was a powerful social technology, driven by mythology, that influenced enough people to actually form proper societies around shared values. Before that book, these stories got twisted and changed every time they were retold from person to person. Once the printing press made mass production possible, the Bible locked those stories in and put everyone on the same page.

Religion plays a different role now than it used to though. We're not sitting around in cabins with nothing but a Bible anymore. We have the internet, access to every perspective on earth, we go to school, we have science filling in gaps that mythology used to fill. People still find value in religion, but it's not the sole foundation of society the way it once was.

I'm actually writing a book about what comes next. About simulation theory and artificial intelligence, the idea that we're externalizing intelligence into silicon and that AI could eventually deliver the things every religion had always promised. And if we're already in a simulation, then maybe those promises were already delivered and we just haven't realized it yet. I've been dreaming about this stuff since 2012 and I want to be an influencer and enjoy the shift and the hype surrounding these events, because I think they're the most important things to happen in the entire unfolding of cosmological history. This moment on this planet is extremely novel.

And honestly when you compare what Christianity was teaching versus what was coming from the other side of that conflict, it's not even close. The Crusades started because Islamic empires had been aggressively expanding for centuries, conquering formerly Christian territories by force. The Quran has explicit instructions for warfare against nonbelievers, it permits things like sex slavery, child marriage, death for apostasy. These aren't cherry picked gotchas, they're central to Sharia law as practiced in multiple countries today.

Christianity had its dark chapters, absolutely.

But the core teachings of Jesus, compassion, forgiveness, turning the other cheek, those are fundamentally different values than what was being spread by conquest by those Islamic countries.

Christians eventually evolved past their violent period. Most Islamic theocracies haven't. There are roughly 50 Muslim-majority countries in the world. The separation of church and state has not occurred for about half of them, those have Sharia-based laws their government enforces, and almost all of them outside of a handful of secular states like Turkey and Albania encourage Sharia at least in family law. Ten countries still punish homosexuality with death. Women's freedoms are severely restricted across much of the Islamic world. Leaving the faith is still a death sentence in some of these places. That's all happening right now.

I'm not saying peaceful Islam doesn't exist, it does, and a lot of individual Muslims are wonderful people living normal lives. But the legal and political systems built on Islamic religious texts are, in practice, far worse than anything modern Christianity produces. And especially back in 1095, the comparison isn't even close.

So my hope in saying all of this, is that people do not only focus on what Christianity did wrong 900 years ago and ignore what the alternative was, and still is in parts of the world, because it's not the full picture.
