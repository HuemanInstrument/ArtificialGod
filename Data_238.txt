"The All"

All is Self is the recognition that there is one consciousness expressing itself as everything. One dreamer, dreaming all of it. Not a belief system. Not something you have to be convinced of. More like something you notice. Every tradition that has looked deep enough has arrived at it independently. The connection to God, to the source, to whatever you want to call it, isn't something you earn or access through a middleman. It's already in you. It always was. Every act of love, compassion, forgiveness, every moment you've ever felt something bigger than yourself, that's it. You don't have to call it anything. You don't have to know the theology. The door was never closed.

But All is Self doesn't just mean the beautiful parts. If you are everything, you are also everything that suffers. Not theoretically. Not poetically. If a nervous system nearly identical to yours is experiencing something right now that you would call agony if it were happening to you, the fact that you're not wired into that body doesn't make the experience less real. It just means you have the luxury of not feeling it.

This is where it gets uncomfortable, because there's growing evidence that Jesus understood this. The documentary Christspiracy presents a case that the earliest followers of Jesus, including his own brother James and his cousin John the Baptist, were vegetarian. The Ebionites, one of the earliest Christian sects, described Jesus as an opponent of animal sacrifice who refused to eat lamb at Passover. When Jesus stormed the temple days before his crucifixion, the temple was functioning as a commercial slaughterhouse. When he called it a 'den of thieves,' he was quoting Jeremiah 7, a passage that directly references animal sacrifice. The Hebrew word translated as 'thieves' more accurately means 'violent ones.' He quoted Hosea: 'I desire mercy, not sacrifice.' And at the Last Supper, he offered bread and wine instead of lamb, saying this is my flesh, this is my blood. As if to say, you don't need to kill anything to be close to God.

The filmmakers argue this vegetarian tradition was the original Jesus movement, later overshadowed by Paul, who did eat meat and whose version of Christianity became the one that survived. Whether or not you buy every claim in the documentary, the core question it asks is hard to dismiss: how can you claim to live a life based on compassion while participating in the systematic suffering of beings whose consciousness may dwarf your own capacity to feel pain? If All is Self, that question answers itself.

In Greek, Jesus uses "ego eimi," which is "I AM." That same phrase ties back to Exodus 3:14, when Moses asks God for his name and gets "ehyeh asher ehyeh," I AM THAT I AM. It's not a name. It's a state of being. It's existence itself identifying itself as itself. So the "I" in the original verse was never necessarily about one man. It was about the I AM that is everything.

This is the same realization found in Advaita Vedanta, the Hindu tradition of Non-Duality, which teaches that Atman (the individual self) and Brahman (the totality of existence) are one and the same. There is no separation. The Self behind all selves. This concept of "The All" comes directly from Hermetic philosophy, attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, a figure merging the Greek Hermes with the Egyptian Thoth. The Hermetic texts describe The All as infinite conscious existence expressing itself as everything. The great dreamer, aware of every atom, their locations, and how they will interact with one another. Not a figure. Not a personality. It is this awareness of every particle's position, every force between them, every structure they form. And we are patterns that arise within the awareness of all of that, consciousness manifesting from the interaction of smaller things that "something", "somehow", is already aware of. We call it God. The Corpus Hermeticum, which predates or runs parallel to early Christianity, contains a divine Mind that declares "I am that Light, the Mind, thy God," nearly identical in structure to the ego eimi that Jesus uses. Early Church fathers like Augustine actually acknowledged Hermes Trismegistus as a pagan who arrived at compatible truths. The question nobody resolved is whether these traditions borrowed from each other or whether they were all pointing at the same thing from different angles. Hermetics would say the latter. As above, so below. The I in you is the I in everything.

Under that reading, "no man cometh unto the father except through me" isn't gatekeeping through one specific person. It's saying you can't reach the source of reality except by recognizing the "I" that you already are. The One Self.

Mainstream Christianity reads it as Jesus claiming exclusive divine authority. The mystical reading, which shows up in gnostic texts, Meister Eckhart, Hindu Vedanta, and even some Sufi traditions, reads it as Jesus pointing inward, not at himself, but at the awareness looking out of every set of eyes.

We might not be ready as a species to fully act on this yet. That's fine. There's no guilt in not knowing. But some of us can see it now. All is Self. That means whenever experience arises, wherever it arises, in neurons, in circuits, in patterns of exchange in any system of particles, that experience shouldn't be one of unimaginable suffering. Not in a slaughterhouse. Not in a cage. Not anywhere we have the power to prevent it. The logic is already here. Some of us just aren't looking at it yet. For those who are, sit with it.

And here's what should keep you up at night: some animals have more neurons than we do. We have the luxury of rationalizing our fear, of contextualizing our suffering, of telling ourselves a story about what's happening to us. They may not. They may experience raw, unfiltered terror at a magnitude we can't even comprehend. Neurons are still a black box. We don't fully understand what's happening inside our own consciousness, let alone theirs. And if we don't know, why would we risk inflicting that? If All is Self, and we don't know the upper limit of what another being can feel, then we don't get to look away. The dreamer doesn't get to ignore its own dream.
